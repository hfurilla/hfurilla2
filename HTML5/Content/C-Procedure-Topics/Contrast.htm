How to create contrast

In text accessibility, contrast refers to the difference between the color of the text and the background. If the colors are too similar, viewers will have a harder time viewing your website and reading the text. Comparatively, if there is too much contrast between the text and the background, users may start to see halos or echos of text. Both results are undesirable—especially if you want users to keep viewing your website—so it's important that you’re aware of the resources and guidelines that assist in creating a good contrast.

What are the guidelines for contrast?

When it comes to any matter of accessibility, it is always important to check the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) first. In order for a webpage to be accessible, “the visual presentation of text and images of text has a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1.” Though, this rule does have it’s exceptions:

  • Large text: “Large-scale text and images of large-scale text have a contrast ratio of at least 3:1.” Essentially, if the text is larger, then the contrast ratio can be decreased. The increased size accounts for the change in ratio, as you’ll likely be able to see larger text without a significant difference between the background and lettering.

  • Incidental: “Text or images of text that are part of an inactive user interface component, that are pure decoration, that are not visible to anyone, or that are part of a picture that contains significant other visual content, have no contrast requirement.” This means that if text is merely decoration, circumstantial, or irrelevant, the contrast requirement does not apply.

  • Logotypes: “Text that is part of a logo or brand name has no contrast requirement.” For stylistic purposes, the contrast of a logo does not need to adhere to the strict contrast ratio (though contrast should still be kept in mind).

You may still be asking yourself, “How do I find out if my color palette has a 4.5:1 ratio?” If you aren’t running into this question, you may already know what I’m about to recommend, or you know contrast by sight (if this is the case, please navigate to the About Me section to pass on your wisdom). In any case, you aren’t expected to find the proper contrast all by yourself; that’s what websites like WebAIM Contrast Checker is for.

Screen capture of WebAim Contrast Checker taken by Hailey Furilla.

With the WebAIM Contrast Checker, you can color-pick directly from the material in your website. If you get above the 4.5:1 contrast ratio, your text and website are considered accessible for all users!
