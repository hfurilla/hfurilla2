Generating Scalable Text

Websites are visited by a wide range of individuals who possess visual disabilities—such as astigmatism, myopia, or hyperopia—and cognitive disabilities—like dyslexia, ADHD, or autism. Remaining aware of the font size, and the coding that relates to it, can reduce the strain and processing difficulties viewers may encounter when reading.

What is scalability?

When designing a website, there is no perfect font size that will accommodate all your viewers. While designers lean towards using 12 pt (or 16px) for body text and 18 pt (or 24 px) for large text, there is no evidence that supports these sizes are adequate for all viewers' experience (). The only way to ensure all viewers can comfortably read on your website is by implementing scalable text.

The official Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (or WCAG) states in their success criterion, “Except for captions and images of text, text can be resized without assistive technology up to 200 percent without loss of content or functionality.” This means that accessible text is judged by its capability to adapt to adjustments made by standard browser features. If it can be increased by 200 percent while remaining interactive, the text is considered accessible.

Screen capture of Google Chrome Zoom feature taken by Hailey Furilla.

If you’ve ever been on a web browser, you’re likely more familiar with this tool than you know. In most cases, it is located at the top left corner of a browser window under “Settings” or “Customize.” This feature, also known as “Zoom,” can increase the webpage’s size by as much as 500 percent. If a webpage is accessible, the page structure will readjust to accommodate the increased text size allowed by the “Zoom” function. If the layout breaks upon zooming in, distorting the information and impacting the functionality of the page, then it would be considered inaccessible. But, how can you ensure that this text is scalable? The answer lies within the code itself.

How to create scalable text

There are a variety of ways you can alter the responsiveness of your text using CSS, a coding language used to style HTML pages (basically, every website you encounter on the internet). Within this styling language, you can change how your text adapts by changing the type of units the text is defined by. Generally, it's best practice to utilize relative units over absolute units.

Absolute units, like pixels, points, or picas, are fixed units, meaning that they remain the same size no matter how much the parent element, or other components of a webpage, change. Simply, they are not scalable. On the other hand, relative units can easily adapt to adjustments, whether they are tied to a parent element or not. There are many different kinds of relative units you can use, both with their own benefits and drawbacks.

  • Named font sizes: These are relative, predefined font sizes determined by the browser. Rather than numbers, these sizes are usually set in terms of ‘small,’ ‘medium,’ or ‘large.’ However, additional values like ‘xx-small’, ‘x-small’, ‘x-large,’ ‘xx-large,’ ‘smaller,’ or ‘larger’ are also accepted. These font sizes are intended to be flexible and scalable. However, it limits coders to a select few sizes, which could pose design restrictions.

  • Percentages and em units: These are measurements relative to the font size of their parent elements. This grants developers far more control over the sizing of their text, as they can choose from a wide range of percentages or numbers to choose the exact sizing they need. Although, these units depend on a parent element, and if it is untethered to such an element, it may automatically be set to the browser’s default. Furthermore, em’s relationship to its parent can result in a cascading effect that multiplies the em values if parent-child tags happen to be stacked within one another.

  • Rem units: Rem, standing for “root em,” are not bound to the root HTML element. This means it bypasses the font size a parent element may set to reduce the possibility of cascading effects and promote consistency throughout your document. For beginners in code, this is an optimal choice for easily meeting accessibility and scalability requirements.

Regardless of what units you choose, it’s important to be mindful of the intended result: the ability to use the “Zoom” tool and size text to 200 percent without losing functionality. Utilizing these relative units will provide you with scalable text and open your website to a wider audience.
